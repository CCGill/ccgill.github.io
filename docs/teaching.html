Reflections

Practical vs. Theoretical Courses

Having spent more than a decade teaching various aspects of mathematics, when I first came to teach practical computational skills there was much to learn.

Many aspects of learning carry across to practical classes - I see learning as something that students must actively engage in. For sufficiently advanced courses, even the best students will find topics and concepts that require practice to master through practical application.

As with any course, the level of the course/student often dictates how much supervision is required, but I found primarily that there are three steps that are critical to learning:

  1. Discovery (which can be guided, through lectures, etc)
  2. guided initial exploration of concepts (direct application in context)
  3. practical application, merging the tools/concepts/ideas with other knowledge - this can be through less directed application.

To retain the knowledge, there is a fourth step:

  1. revision/re-use.

At the end of the day, putting a concept or tool to use drastically increases the chances you retain it in the longer term.
